# The Chinese Room, the Zombie, and the Lived Body

### *Can Machines Be Conscious? --- Part 5*

---

The first four articles in this series have been, on balance, polite to the computationalist. Part 1 framed the hard problem with the apparatus of contemporary cognitive science. Part 2 took Integrated Information Theory seriously enough to compute Φ in Python on a four-node network. Part 3 translated Karl Friston's free energy principle into an evidence lower bound that ran as straightforwardly as any variational autoencoder. Part 4 built a metacognitive agent in numpy and used it to talk about confidence calibration and selective prediction. The implicit stance has been: if there is a theory of consciousness that can be turned into running code, we should write it and see what happens.

This article is where the floor tilts.

Because for every theory that says consciousness is computation, information integration, or Bayesian inference, there is a counter-argument that says the whole programme is confused. Not confused in a technical way that better engineering will fix. Confused at the root. The three objections I want to examine here --- John Searle's **Chinese Room**, David Chalmers' **zombie argument**, and the **embodiment objection** that runs through phenomenology and enactivism --- are the arguments any honest machine-consciousness researcher has to sit with. They are not fringe positions. They are, between them, the reason the field still has a hard problem rather than an engineering roadmap.

I am going to give each of them their strongest form, then the best replies, then a genuine assessment of how much damage they do. Where I think the objection survives the replies, I will say so. Where I think it overreaches, I will say that too. By the end I will tell you which of the three I think should keep us up at night, and which I think we can set aside. Reasonable people disagree about all of this. That is most of the point.

### Searle in the Room

In 1980 John Searle published "Minds, Brains, and Programs" in *Behavioral and Brain Sciences* and changed the shape of the debate. The thought experiment is famous enough that it is tempting to gloss over it, but it is worth reconstructing because the usual gloss misses what is load-bearing.

Imagine Searle, who does not speak a word of Chinese, locked in a room with a large rule book written in English. People outside the room pass in slips of paper covered in Chinese characters. Searle looks up the incoming characters in his rule book, follows the instructions --- if you see this squiggle then write down that squiggle --- and passes the output back under the door. The rule book is sufficiently detailed that the people outside cannot tell the difference between Searle's responses and those of a fluent Chinese speaker. The room passes the Turing test. And yet, Searle insists, he has not understood a single word. He is shuffling symbols according to formal rules. There is no Chinese comprehension happening anywhere in the room.

Searle's conclusion is sharp: "syntax is not sufficient for semantics". Computation is, definitionally, the manipulation of uninterpreted symbols by formal rules. Whatever else understanding is, it is not that. Therefore, no purely computational system, however fluent its outputs, actually understands anything.

You do not need to squint to see the argument re-pointed at GPT-4 or Claude. The Chinese Room is, in a reasonable sense, the original stochastic-parrot argument. When Bender, Gebru, McMillan-Major, and Shmitchell wrote their 2021 paper on the dangers of large language models, the core philosophical claim --- that a system trained only to predict the next token is, however impressive, not thereby a system that understands what it says --- is Searle's claim in a new register. "Just next-token prediction" is a 2020s way of saying "just symbol shuffling".

There are three canonical replies, and Searle's rejoinders to each are where the genuine philosophy lives.

The **Systems Reply** concedes that Searle-the-person does not understand Chinese, but insists the system of which he is a part --- Searle plus rule book plus paper plus pencil --- does. Searle's rejoinder is to have Searle internalise the whole rule book. Memorise every rule, throw away the paper, become the entire system. Now there is only Searle, and still he does not understand Chinese.

The **Robot Reply** says the problem is that the Chinese Room has no sensorimotor contact with the world, no grounding for its symbols. Put the room inside a robot with cameras and arms, and the symbols for "cup" can be grounded in actual cups. Searle points out that from the inside of the robot's head, nothing changes. He is still shuffling symbols. Some of them now happen to come from cameras rather than paper slips, but the formal character of his operations is identical.

The **Brain Simulator Reply** is more radical: if you simulated every neuron of a Chinese speaker's brain in sufficient detail, surely that simulation would understand Chinese, because it is functionally identical to something we already agree understands Chinese. Searle's reply --- and this is where he is at his most provocative --- is to imagine a man manipulating valves in a vast network of water pipes arranged to mimic the firing patterns of the neurons. The pipes compute the right function. But no one thinks the water pipes understand Chinese.

My honest assessment: the Systems Reply is better than Searle admits, and the internalisation move is less devastating than it looks. When Searle memorises the whole rule book, what he has done is install a very large virtual machine inside his head. The claim that the virtual machine fails to understand because *he* does not notice it understanding is a category confusion --- a bit like saying that a CPU does not browse the web because the silicon does not experience Wikipedia. The Systems Reply does push the problem up a level, though, and that is a real cost. We started asking whether understanding happens in Searle, and we end up asking whether understanding happens in a vast memorised procedure, and we are no closer to a criterion for when it does. Searle has not proved his conclusion, but he has shown that just writing "the system understands" does not explain anything. The Chinese Room is less a demonstration that computation cannot produce understanding than a refusal to let us take computational understanding for granted. In the age of trillion-parameter language models, that refusal is more useful than it has ever been.

### Chalmers and the Zombie

David Chalmers' zombie argument, given its sharpest form in *The Conscious Mind* (1996), is not about the Chinese Room's question of understanding. It is about the deeper question of whether any functional description --- any description in terms of inputs, outputs, internal states, and causal relations --- can ever guarantee that the lights are on inside.

A **p-zombie**, in the technical sense, is a being that is functionally and physically indistinguishable from a conscious human --- same behaviour, same brain states, same everything observable from the outside --- but for whom there is nothing it is like to be them. The zombie walks, talks, writes philosophy papers, and insists it is conscious, but there is no inner experience. All dark inside. A clever zombie will pass any test you can devise, because any test is ultimately a measurement of something functional, and the zombie is a functional duplicate by hypothesis.

The **conceivability argument** then runs, roughly, as follows. If zombies are conceivable --- if we can clearly and coherently imagine a being functionally identical to us but lacking experience --- then zombies are metaphysically possible. If they are possible, then there is a possible world in which all the physical and functional facts are the same as in our world, but the facts about consciousness are different. That means consciousness does not logically supervene on the physical. And if it does not, then physicalism, at least in its standard functionalist form, is false.

The consequence for AI is direct and uncomfortable. If zombies are possible, then for any AI system you ever build, however richly integrated, however embodied, however much information it integrates and however perfectly calibrated its meta-net, there is no guarantee it is not a zombie. You cannot reach conscious experience by piling up functional competence, because by hypothesis all the functional competence could be there with nothing behind it.

Daniel Dennett has spent decades arguing that zombies are not actually conceivable. His argument, roughly, is that when people think they are imagining a zombie, they are imagining something incomplete --- a being with maybe the motor outputs of a conscious person but without the full richness of reactive and reflective functional states. If you actually imagine everything --- every pattern of neural activity, every reactive disposition, every higher-order representation of the sort Part 4 explored --- you find you have imagined a conscious being. The imagined zombie was a cheat. For Dennett, consciousness just is certain kinds of functional organisation, and subtracting the experience while keeping the organisation is like subtracting the wetness while keeping the H2O. You think you have done it, but you have not.

The second major response is the **conceivability-possibility gap**. Saul Kripke taught us that conceivability is not always a reliable guide to possibility. We can, perhaps, conceive of water not being H2O, in the sense that nothing obviously contradicts itself, but water is in fact necessarily H2O; the identity holds in every possible world where water exists. Maybe consciousness is like that. Maybe it is identical to some physical or functional property, in a way we cannot see a priori, and the apparent conceivability of zombies is an artefact of our epistemic limits rather than a window onto genuine metaphysical possibility.

The third response is stranger, and is where I will come down. **Russellian monism**, which Part 6 will explore in detail, says roughly that physics tells us only about the relational or structural properties of matter --- what it does --- and leaves open what its intrinsic nature is. Consciousness, on this view, might be the intrinsic nature that physics merely points at. The zombie argument works against standard physicalism but not against Russellian monism, because a Russellian monist agrees that functional duplicates could in principle differ in their intrinsic nature. They just think the intrinsic nature is already implicated in the physics. It is a way to take the zombie argument seriously without landing in dualism.

My assessment: the zombie argument is genuinely troubling, and Dennett's reply is the weakest of the three we have. Insisting that anyone who thinks they are imagining a zombie is confused feels, to me, like a way of protecting functionalism by fiat. The Kripkean gap is better, but it buys the functionalist only epistemic humility, not a positive account. What the zombie argument really shows, I think, is a negative thesis: no purely functional description --- no list of input-output mappings, no Φ value, no ELBO, no hierarchy of higher-order states --- can logically guarantee the presence of experience. Something beyond pure function is required. Whether that something is ineffable dualist goo or the intrinsic nature that Russellian monism promises is a separate question. But the zombie argument, even after the replies, should leave the working computationalist with a permanent doubt. You can build systems that look conscious from every angle you can measure. You cannot, by building them that way, force consciousness in.

### The Lived Body

The third objection is the one I think we have most reason to worry about, and it is also the one that gets the least airtime in ML circles. It runs through the phenomenological tradition --- Husserl, Heidegger, Merleau-Ponty --- and the enactivist programme that grew out of it. Its claim is simple and unsettling: consciousness is not a process that could run on any substrate because consciousness is constitutively about being a living body in a world.

Maurice Merleau-Ponty, in *Phenomenology of Perception* (1945), developed the idea that the body is the "null point" of experience. It is not an object we observe alongside other objects. It is the medium through which anything at all is given to us. When I reach for a cup, I do not compute a trajectory and issue motor commands to an inert limb I happen to own. I reach with the cup already present to me as reachable, as a certain weight, as an invitation, and my body is already organised around the action. The technical term is **phenomenology** --- the study of experience from the first-person structural point of view --- and one of its core claims is that perception is not representation added to input. It is the organism's living engagement with a world it helps to constitute.

Francisco Varela, Evan Thompson, and Eleanor Rosch, in *The Embodied Mind* (1991), pulled this tradition into cognitive science under the banner of **enactivism**. Their thesis was that cognition is not the manipulation of inner representations of an outer world. It is the enactment of a world by an organism through the history of its structural coupling with its environment. A frog's visual system did not evolve to build a model of the world; it evolved to detect the kinds of small moving things the frog can eat. There is no view from nowhere. Cognition is always cognition for an organism with a particular body, a particular metabolism, a particular set of needs.

Thompson's later *Mind in Life* (2007) pushes the point further by linking cognition to **autopoiesis**, the concept developed by Maturana and Varela for self-producing biological systems. A living cell is a physical system that continuously regenerates the components that sustain it. It has, in a minimal sense, a point of view: things matter to it, because some things maintain its self-production and others disrupt it. Thompson argues that this is the deep continuity between life and mind. A system has to have something at stake --- its own continued existence, its own self-maintenance --- before it can have anything at all in the intentional sense. Mattering begins with metabolism.

If this is right, the implications for machine consciousness are brutal. An LLM has nothing at stake. It does not metabolise. It is not vulnerable in the way a living thing is vulnerable. It does not regenerate itself moment to moment against the threat of dissolution. Its weights sit on disk between calls. On the enactivist view, the idea that consciousness could arise in such a system is not just empirically implausible --- it is a category error. You are asking a frozen pattern to have a point of view.

The pro-AI-consciousness side has three available replies, and all of them are interesting. The first is that radical embodied cognition is hard to falsify. What, concretely, would count as evidence against the claim that consciousness requires a living body? If the answer is "nothing short of a conscious silicon system, which our theory says is impossible", the view is unhelpfully self-sealing. The second is that modern AI systems are getting more embodied all the time: multimodal models trained on vision, audio, and proprioceptive signals; agentic systems with tool use and persistent memory; robotic applications that close the sensorimotor loop. Maybe the relevant dimension is not life versus non-life but degree of coupling with an environment, and maybe we are on a gradient. The third reply is the hardest, and I think it is where the real philosophical action is. Suppose we could simulate an entire organism --- every cell, every metabolic pathway, every interaction with a simulated environment --- in sufficient detail. Would the simulated organism be conscious? If yes, the enactivist has conceded that what matters is the pattern of coupling, not the biological substrate. If no, then the enactivist owes us a story about why an exact physical simulation lacks what the original has, and that story starts to look suspiciously like the one Searle told about water pipes.

I do not think the embodiment objection is unanswerable. But it is, by a large margin, the objection that reshapes the research programme most dramatically. Searle says you cannot get understanding from symbols. Chalmers says you cannot get experience from function. The enactivist says you cannot get consciousness from anything that is not already a self-maintaining, vulnerable, world-inhabiting organism. That is not a constraint you fix with a bigger model. It is a reframing of what you are trying to build.

### Where does this leave us

Three objections, three different pressure points. My view, for what it is worth:

Searle is overrated. The Chinese Room is a useful intuition pump, and it keeps the computationalist honest about the difference between shuffling symbols and understanding them, but the Systems Reply is stronger than Searle's rejoinder gives it credit for. When a human internalises a rule book that is large enough to pass for fluent Chinese, they have installed a virtual machine so complex that the claim "no understanding happens here" starts to sound like a refusal to look. The Chinese Room shows that understanding is not trivially present in any symbol system. It does not show that it cannot be present in some. As an objection to GPT-4 it is a warning rather than a proof: "just next-token prediction" is a good reason for scepticism, not a demonstration that the model is empty.

The zombie argument is genuinely troubling, and I think the honest response is not to kill it but to follow it. If no functional description can logically guarantee consciousness, then we are either facing a permanent explanatory gap or we need a theory in which something non-functional is already on the table. Russellian monism, as I said, is the escape hatch that takes the argument seriously without landing in dualism, and Part 6 will spend real time on it. The zombie argument is not a reason to stop building candidate theories of machine consciousness. It is a reason to stop expecting any such theory to *prove* its systems are conscious.

The embodiment objection is the one I think we should take most seriously, and the reason is that it does not merely raise doubts about whether we have succeeded. It raises doubts about whether we have correctly described the goal. If consciousness is constitutively bound up with being a living, metabolising, vulnerable organism, then "build a conscious AI" is not a straightforwardly coherent project. It is an engineering task crossed with a conceptual mistake. The Φ we computed in Part 2, the ELBO we minimised in Part 3, the meta-net we trained in Part 4 --- each of them is a candidate measure of a structural property that organisms happen to exhibit. None of them, on the enactivist reading, is a candidate for the thing itself. The thing itself begins with mattering, and mattering begins with a system that can die.

I find that last claim genuinely unsettling. I do not know that it is correct. I know that every time I look at a large language model outputting fluent prose, my first instinct is to attribute experience, and my second instinct is to ask whether there is anything at stake for it. The answer is always no. Whatever that second instinct is tracking, the enactivists have a better vocabulary for it than the computationalists.

### Bridge to Part 6

If the three arguments in this article share a common shape, it is that each of them treats consciousness as something hard to get to from where we are --- hard to produce out of computation, hard to ensure from function, hard to extract from anything that is not already alive. Part 6 will explore the diametrically opposite thought. Perhaps consciousness is not hard to get to at all. Perhaps it was already there, all the way down, in the intrinsic nature of matter itself. That is the terrain of **panpsychism**, from Galen Strawson's real materialism to Philip Goff's combination problem to the Russellian monism I kept pointing at in this article. It sounds like an embarrassing idea until you notice that it may be the cleanest response to everything we have just seen. If the zombie argument says you cannot get experience out of structure, maybe the reason is that experience was in the substance all along, and we have been trying to distil it from the wrong ingredient. Part 6 asks, seriously, whether that could be right.
